# The Role of Touch in Memorization: Digital vs. Paper Mediums

This project investigates whether **digital or paper mediums influence short-term memorization performance**. The study explores how the increasing reliance on digital tools in education—especially after the COVID-19 pandemic—affects learning outcomes and memory recall. 

### Research Question

* Does the **medium used for studying (paper vs. digital)** affect memorization performance?
* Does **interaction with the learning medium** influence how well information is remembered? 

### Hypothesis

The researchers hypothesized that **paper would lead to better memorization** because it is more tangible and encourages more physical interaction with the material. 

### Methodology

**Participants**

* 60 undergraduate students (ages 18–25)
* Randomly assigned to either a **paper group (30)** or **digital group (30)**. 

**Procedure**

1. Participants were given a list of **12 neutral words** to memorize for **45 seconds**.
2. They completed a **word search distraction task** to simulate real-world interruptions.
3. Participants then performed a **verbal recall test**, reporting as many words as they remembered. 

**Measured Variables**

* **Dependent Variable:** Number of words correctly recalled.
* **Independent Variable:** Study medium (paper vs. digital).
* **Additional Measure:** Number of **interactions with the medium** (e.g., pointing, scrolling). 

### Key Findings

#### 1. Medium Type

There was **no statistically significant difference** in memorization between paper and digital mediums (p = 0.62). Participants memorized approximately the same number of words in both conditions. 

#### 2. Interaction with the Medium

Although the medium itself did not affect memorization, **interaction with the material showed a weak positive correlation with memory performance**. Participants who interacted more with the content tended to recall more words. 

### Key Takeaways

* Paper and digital mediums are **equally effective for short-term memorization**.
* **Active engagement with study materials** is more important than the medium itself.
* Encouraging interaction (e.g., pointing, scrolling, annotating) may improve learning outcomes. 

### Limitations

* Convenience sampling may have introduced bias (e.g., participants from a choir group with strong memorization skills).
* Experiments were conducted in **different environments and times**, which could affect participant performance.
* Some participants may have been **tired or distracted**, potentially influencing results. 

### Implications

These findings suggest that educators and students can confidently use **either digital or paper materials** without negatively affecting memorization. Instead, teaching strategies should focus on **encouraging active interaction with learning materials** to improve memory retention.
